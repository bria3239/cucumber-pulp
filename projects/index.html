Deconstructing the Prison Industrial Complex
in New York City Subways

A hot take to go with some pictures I took for this assignment.

Bria Miller | Dec. 15th 2024

The prison industrial complex is a system of things we think of properly as jails and cops, but in reality, is a sprawling, life-destroying system that extends far beyond these institutions and permeates all aspects of our lives. In New York City, surveillance technology, uniformed personnel, and weaponized architecture expands the carceral state and increases arrested mobility of Black and marginalized groups.

The city has long been a testing ground for these mechanisms of social control, which are used as a crutch for safety and protection, to obscure a real, holistic, and more systemic approach to societal problems.

One of the largest and most brutal occupying gangs in the country, the NYPD, is expanding its surveillance of New Yorkers. This past spring, Mayor Eric Adams stuffed 1,000 cops in NYC subway stations, and in 2023 alone the NYPD recorded more stops than it had in nearly a decade.

In a coordinated effort with Mayor Adams, NY Governor Kathy Hochul placed an additional 1,000 members of the State Police, the National Guard and the transportation authority to conduct bag checks in the cityâ€™s busiest stations

An Amnesty International investigation reveals that the NYPD has the ability to track people in Manhattan, Brooklyn and the Bronx by running images from 15,280 surveillance cameras into invasive and discriminatory facial recognition software. Surveillance technologies bolster state power and enable NYPD to act as an overseer, tracking New Yorkers as they move through the city.

In an economy built on 500 years of harm and violence toward a community that was stolen from its homeland and brought to build this country, it is no surprise that the colonizers would choose to turn around and charge the descendants of those who were stolen and dispossessed to move across stolen land.

One of the most ubiquitous tools of control in the making of the NYC subway system are the fare gates and turnstiles. Like the NYPD "overseer," I see these structures as mirroring certain functions of the plantation, maintaining a system of confinement, control. These gates are more visible manifestations of the prison industrial complex that many of us encounter daily. Not only do they obstruct our right to freely move across the land we inhabit, but represent a rapidly growing economy centered around designing the arrested mobility of poor and working class people.

In 2023, The MTA installed its first harder-to-jump fare gates as part of a test of potential designs aimed at reducing the fare-evasion. However, the millions of dollars invested in mobility-restricting infrastructure would be far better spent on programs that proactively promote personal and communal safety.

As we critically examine the implications of carceral infrastructure throughout New York City, it's essential to recognize that the trends seen here are mirrored in many other metropolitan areas across the country, such as Washington D.C. As of Fall 2024, in an attempt to combat fare evasion, the city has installed over 1,200 five-foot-tall gates and taller fences at all 98 stations in the Metrorail system.

These practices, rather than keep us safe and further expand the carceral state and criminalize Black, brown, poor and working class people.
